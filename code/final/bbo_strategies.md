# Which optimisation strategies led to your strongest results, and why were they effective? How did these strategies influence your decisions as the challenge progressed?

Three strategies produced my strongest results across the thirteen rounds of this project, and each one built on the failures of what came before.

The first and most impactful was systematic hyperparameter tuning of the Gaussian Process surrogate model, which I implemented in round 7. I tested 48 configurations comprising 8 kernel types crossed with 6 noise parameter values, evaluated using 3-fold cross-validation. This revealed that Rational Quadratic and Matérn 2.5 (including ARD variants) were the two strongest kernel families, each winning on 3 of 8 functions. ARD was critical for the 8-dimensional function, where Matérn 2.5 ARD achieved the best RMSE. The optimal noise parameter was bimodal across functions, with some preferring very small values (1e-8) and others preferring larger values (1e-3), rather than clustering in a narrow range. The worst-to-best RMSE ratio reached 5.7× on some functions, confirming that default hyperparameters can be substantially suboptimal. This was effective because it replaced guesswork with evidence. In earlier rounds I had been adjusting exploration parameters and trying entirely different models without first ensuring that my base surrogate was well-calibrated. The analogy I keep returning to is that aggressive exploration with a poor surrogate approximates random search, whereas moderate exploration with a well-calibrated surrogate is genuinely informative. This single insight reshaped every decision I made from round 7 onward.

The second strategy was using the ARD length scales from the tuned GP to perform dimension reduction across all functions, not only the high-dimensional ones. The ARD analysis revealed clear relevance structure: for function 8 (8 dimensions), 4 dimensions had comparatively short length scales while the remaining 4 had very large length scales, with one hitting sklearn's upper bound of 1e5, confirming near-total irrelevance. Similar patterns appeared throughout — function 7 (6D) reduced to 3 effective dimensions, function 6 (5D) to 3, and even the 2-dimensional functions 1 and 2 reduced to effectively 1 dimension each. I fixed the irrelevant dimensions at their best known values and searched only in the reduced space. This was effective because it addressed the fundamental challenge of the project, namely that sparse data cannot meaningfully characterize a high-dimensional landscape. One important caveat is that function 4 (4D) had nearly uniform length scales (2.58 to 2.99), making the median-based split somewhat arbitrary — in such cases the dimension reduction may not reflect genuine structure but rather a lack of signal. The broader caveat, which I documented in round 10, is that dimension pruning is an irreversible strategic commitment. If the data happened to show little variation along a dimension that is actually important but was never sampled at informative locations, my reduction could systematically exclude relevant features. I accepted this risk because the alternative, searching the full space with sparse data, was demonstrably unproductive.

The third strategy was replacing grid-based acquisition function evaluation with a combination of gradient ascent on the GP surrogate and Expected Improvement maximization via multi-start L-BFGS-B, cross-checked against the UCB acquisition function. In early rounds I had evaluated the GP predictions and acquisition function across millions of grid points, which required parallel computation on 12 to 192 CPU cores and produced gigabytes of stored results. The optimisation-based approach ran in seconds, produced continuous rather than grid-discretized candidates, and scaled gracefully to higher dimensions. I implemented this fully in round 10 and it immediately became the backbone of my pipeline. In practice, gradient ascent won on 5 of 8 functions — typically the maximization problems where it could exploit the GP mean surface directly — while EI won on 3 functions, notably both minimization problems and the highest-dimensional function (8D). UCB never produced the best candidate, confirming its role as a cross-check rather than a primary acquisition strategy. The pipeline predicted improvement on 5 of 8 functions overall, with the low-dimensional functions already well-exploited and showing no further gains.

These three strategies influenced my subsequent decisions by establishing a clear priority order. First ensure the surrogate model is well-calibrated, then identify which dimensions matter, then search efficiently in the reduced space. Whenever I was tempted to try a new method or add complexity, I evaluated it against this pipeline and asked whether it would improve any of these three steps.


# In your view, what qualities define a successful strategy? Is it outcomes alone, or also adaptability, reasoning or efficiency?

Outcomes matter, but they are insufficient as the sole measure of success, particularly in a setting with limited query budgets and high-dimensional uncertainty. A strategy that stumbles upon a good result by chance teaches nothing and cannot be reliably reproduced or transferred to new problems.

I believe a successful strategy has four qualities. The first is sound reasoning, meaning that each decision follows logically from evidence and explicit assumptions. My round 7 tuning was successful largely because it was systematic rather than intuitive. The second is adaptability, meaning the ability to recognize when an approach is failing and change course. My most costly failure was persisting with grid search for nine rounds despite clear evidence of diminishing returns. The eventual pivot to gradient-based search was an act of adaptability, but it came far too late. The third is efficiency, meaning achieving results proportional to the resources invested. Spending two hours on 192 cores to evaluate a grid that could be replaced by a few seconds of gradient optimization is a failure of efficiency regardless of the outcome. The fourth is transparency, meaning that the strategy can be documented clearly enough for another researcher to reproduce it. My round 10 pipeline, consisting of fit the tuned GP, extract length scales, prune dimensions, run gradient ascent, cross-check with UCB, satisfies this criterion because each step is deterministic given the model and data.

If I had to rank these, I would place reasoning first because it is the foundation for the other three. Adaptability without reasoning is just randomness. Efficiency without reasoning is premature optimization. And transparency without reasoning is mere documentation of arbitrary choices.


How could the strategies you identified be applied or adapted to professional ML/AI projects beyond the BBO capstone project?

The three core strategies I identified, namely surrogate model calibration, automatic dimension reduction, and efficient search, transfer almost directly to several professional contexts.

In drug discovery and materials science, researchers routinely face expensive black-box evaluations where each experiment costs thousands of dollars or takes weeks. Bayesian optimization with well-tuned GP surrogates is already standard practice in these fields, and the lesson that surrogate quality matters more than exploration aggressiveness applies directly. A pharmaceutical company running high-throughput screening with limited compound libraries faces exactly the same trade-off between exploring chemical space broadly and exploiting promising molecular scaffolds.

In hyperparameter optimization for production ML systems, the same framework applies. Tools like Optuna and Ax implement sequential Bayesian optimization to tune model hyperparameters efficiently. The ARD-based dimension reduction I used maps onto the common observation that most hyperparameters have negligible effect on model performance while a few are critical. Identifying which hyperparameters matter and focusing the search on those is directly analogous to what my ARD analysis accomplished for the BBO functions.

More broadly, the meta-lesson about matching method complexity to data availability applies to nearly every ML project. In consulting and industry settings, I have seen teams invest weeks building complex deep learning pipelines when a well-tuned gradient boosted tree would have sufficed for their data size. The discipline of starting simple, validating thoroughly, and adding complexity only when justified by evidence is perhaps the most transferable skill from this project.

The lesson about algorithmic efficiency versus computational power also has wide professional relevance. It is almost always cheaper and faster to improve an algorithm than to rent more compute. My experience of migrating to 192 cloud cores to run a fundamentally inefficient grid search is a cautionary example that I expect to recall whenever I am tempted to throw hardware at a problem that actually requires a better approach.


# What successful strategies did you notice in your peers' approaches, and what made them effective?

One approach I found particularly interesting among my peers was the use of Expected Improvement as the primary acquisition function from early rounds rather than UCB. While I spent several rounds manually tuning the beta parameter in UCB, peers who adopted EI avoided this issue entirely because EI naturally balances exploration and exploitation without requiring a tunable parameter. This meant they could focus their effort on surrogate model quality and search strategy rather than on acquisition function calibration. The effectiveness of this choice reinforced my own eventual conclusion that reducing the number of things you need to tune is itself a valuable strategy.

I also observed peers who used established Bayesian optimization libraries like BoTorch from the outset. Their results were often competitive from the first round because they benefited from well-tested implementations of sequential BO with gradient-based acquisition optimization, proper handling of numerical edge cases, and efficient GP fitting. In contrast, my from-scratch approach introduced subtle implementation issues that took rounds to identify and resolve. The trade-off is that library users may have gained less deep understanding of the underlying mechanics, but for a competition measured on outcomes, their efficiency was clearly advantageous.

Another strategy I noticed was the use of multi-start optimization for acquisition function maximization, where peers launched gradient-based searches from dozens or hundreds of random starting points to avoid local optima. This is something I arrived at only in my later rounds and wish I had adopted earlier. It addresses the fundamental risk that any single gradient ascent trajectory might converge to a local rather than global maximum of the acquisition function.

I see significant overlap between my final strategy and these peer approaches in that we all converged on GP-based surrogates with principled acquisition function optimization. The differences were primarily in how quickly we arrived there and how much effort was spent on detours along the way.


# What suggestions or perspectives could strengthen your peers' strategies, and how do their reflections broaden your view of what success means in optimisation?

For peers who relied heavily on library implementations, I would suggest that investing some time in understanding what happens inside the library, particularly how the GP kernel is selected, how the acquisition function is optimized, and how numerical stability is maintained, could help them diagnose problems when the library defaults are not appropriate for a specific function. My round 7 tuning showed that default hyperparameters can be substantially suboptimal, and a practitioner who cannot look inside the black box of their own optimization tool may miss opportunities for significant improvement.

For peers who focused primarily on surrogate model accuracy, I would highlight that acquisition function optimization deserves equal attention. A perfect surrogate paired with a coarse grid search over the acquisition function can underperform a decent surrogate paired with efficient gradient-based acquisition optimization. The bottleneck shifts as the project progresses, and recognizing when to shift focus from model quality to search efficiency is an important skill.

My peers' reflections have broadened my view of success in one important way. Several participants achieved competitive results with straightforward implementations that they deployed early and refined incrementally, rather than pursuing the breadth of methods I explored. Their success demonstrates that consistency and focus can outperform ambition and variety. In a professional context, the practitioner who delivers a well-tuned simple solution on schedule is often more valuable than the one who promises a sophisticated solution that arrives late and undertested. This is a humbling but practically important lesson that I take forward from this experience.
